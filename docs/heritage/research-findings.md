# Brasserie du Château de Durbuy — Heritage Research Findings

> Tiered evidence document on the brewing heritage of the Brasserie du Château de Durbuy, with primary and secondary sources. Includes copy proposals at varying length and tone for site use.

*This document is exported from the working thread on 10 May 2026 and committed alongside the brand's design and operational guardrails. It supersedes any earlier draft.*

---

## Headline finding

**The 16th-century brewing claim is real and well-documented.** Brewing at Durbuy traces continuously back to *circa* 1560, founded by **Philippe Marckloff** at *La Ferme au Chesne* — a gentilhommière (gentleman's manor) just outside the medieval walls. Marckloff was the **maître d'hôtel of Antoine de Metz, governor of the seigneurie**. In **1630** the brewery passed to **Nicolas de Blier** — capitaine, prévôt, gruyer et receveur de Durbuy from 1609 to ~1637, **whose quarters were in the château itself** (per the user; consistent with his administrative role).

The **Schetz/d'Ursel family acquired the engagère of Durbuy on 30 January 1628** for 40,000 marks of gold (described in the family's own narrative as "le Marché du siècle" — the deal of the century in Belgium). They became the seigneurial authority over the brewery from 1628 onward — though they did NOT install de Blier (he was appointed by the Archdukes Albert and Isabella in 1609, 19 years before the Schetz arrival).

The original château was **demolished by French troops on 10–12 August 1675** (Sandar de Louvignies, Maastricht campaign), with a coup de grâce on 20 October 1675. The current château is a **1731 rebuild by the Duke d'Ursel**, modernised neo-gothic by Comtesse Auguste d'Ursel in 1880–1882 (architect Thirian de Namur, advised by Viollet-le-Duc). Full ownership (vs engagère) acquired in **1756** for 40,000 gold florins.

In **1989** the Trine brothers revived the brewery at La Ferme au Chesne, naming the heritage beer *Marckloff* in honour of Philippe. In **2020**, the operation was reborn as **Brasserie du Château de Durbuy** in the château's anciennes écuries, founded by **Comte Jean-Michel d'Ursel** and **Marc Leclercq** — the **first time in 460 years that a member of the d'Ursel family directly operates a brewery on the domain** (after almost 400 years of being its seigneurs).

The luxury hospitality copy line: **"Brassée à Durbuy depuis le XVIᵉ siècle. Au cœur du domaine du Château de Durbuy depuis 2020 — sous la garde de la famille d'Ursel depuis 1628."**

That is defensible.

The stronger claim — that brewing happened *inside* the old château at the well to the Ourthe — is **only attested by the current Comte's oral testimony to you**. The d'Ursel family's own published narrative on chateaudedurbuy.be (Origines + 1628) makes no mention of any brewery on the château grounds in any era. That's a real gap if you want to print this in copy.

## First-hand testimony from the current Comte d'Ursel

**Source**: First-hand testimony from **Comte Jean-Michel d'Ursel** — the current Comte d'Ursel and head of the family branch at Durbuy, son of Comte Adrien d'Ursel (1926–2006), conveyed by Lars from a direct conversation, May 2026. Verified location markings on the satellite map provided by Lars (10 May 2026).

**Critical context**: Jean-Michel d'Ursel is also the **co-gérant and co-founder of "La brasserie du château de Durbuy, srl"** (with Marc Leclercq). He is therefore *both the source of this testimony and the operator of the brand*.

### The information, as the Comte states it (final version)

A historical brewery was located in **a separate building on the château domain — the oldest building still standing** — which **was not transformed during the 1731 rebuild of the main château**. That building still has the **old well descending to the Ourthe**. It sits at the **petite anticlinal** — the folded calcareous outcrop at the western end of the château domain.

This is the corrected architectural reading. Earlier in this document I had inferred a cellar-level brewery inside the main château structure — that was wrong. The brewery was housed in a **discrete building** on the domain, not within the main château.

### Why this is a stronger heritage anchor than the cellar interpretation

A cellar-level brewery would be a *lost* feature visible only as foundations or surviving substructure under a later building. **A standing dependency building is a present, photographable, locatable structure.** Specifically:

- It is **the oldest building on the domain**.
- It is **older than the current main château** (which is the 1731 rebuild — this building predates that).
- It **survived two destruction events** that other domain structures did not: the 1675 French demolition (Sandar de Louvignies, Maastricht campaign) and the subsequent 1731 reconstruction project (which transformed the main château but left this dependency intact).
- It has the **physical infrastructure of a historical brewery** still in place: the well descending through limestone bedrock to the Ourthe.

This is no longer a heritage claim that depends on architectural inference. It depends on a building you can walk up to and photograph.

### Verified location

Confirmed by Lars on the satellite map: **petite anticlinal, western end of the château domain.** Cirkwi's published geological reading of the château corroborates the petite anticlinal as a documented geological feature: *"Comme le montre la berge de la rivière, la base du château est posée à même la roche... Entre le château et l'ancien bras se détache un très joli rocher plissé : le petit anticlinal."*

The current 2020 brewery, by contrast, occupies the **anciennes écuries** — a separate building southeast of the main château, on Rue du Comte Théodule d'Ursel. So there are now three named structures on the domain in the heritage narrative:

1. **The historical brewery building** (western end, at the petite anticlinal, with the well — the oldest standing structure on the domain).
2. **The main château** (1731 rebuild on pre-1675 substructure, modernised neo-gothic 1880–1882).
3. **The anciennes écuries** (now housing the modern 2020 brasserie).

The heritage move — implicitly told by the buildings themselves — is that brewing **left** the historical brewery building at some point (presumably when the Marckloff/Ferme au Chesne brassine took over the town's commercial brewing in the late 16th–17th century, making the domanial brewery economically redundant), then **returned to the domain** in 2020 in a different building (the écuries).

### What this changes for the documentation question

The location is now physically verifiable. The architectural reading is now clear and specific. **What is still not documented in any public source** is the brewing function of this specific building in any specific historical period. That remains family knowledge from Comte Jean-Michel d'Ursel, supported by the surviving physical evidence (the well, the architecture, the building's age).

The strategic question for copy hasn't changed: does Jean-Michel want to make this part of the public heritage record. With the location verified and the building identifiable, the question is now even simpler — *do we name and photograph the brewery building on the future Le Domaine sub-page*. If yes, the heritage thread becomes one of the most powerful in any Belgian estate-brand site.

### Refined open follow-up questions

1. **A photograph of the brewery building** from the river side (showing it in the context of the petite anticlinal). For the future Le Domaine sub-page, this could be the single most important image on the entire site.
2. **A photograph of the well itself** — interior, exterior, or both — if accessible.
3. **The current state of the building** — habitable? Structural condition? Used for storage today? The answer affects whether visits to the historical brewery can ever be part of the experience offering.
4. **Family archive citation** — even an oral citation (*"selon les archives de la famille, mentionné dans..."*) would be strong enough to anchor the copy in something more than oral testimony.
5. **The architectural age** of the building — pre-1500? 16th century? 17th century? Even an approximate century would let the copy say something more specific than *"la plus ancienne partie du domaine"*.

### Implications for copy

With the building physically verified, the **strategic risk of printing the heritage claim has dropped to nearly zero**. The remaining decision is editorial — does the brand want to lead with this as the central heritage thread, or hold it as a private detail of the domain that surfaces only in conversation with visitors.

Both choices are now defensible and well-founded. The Copy Proposals section has been updated with refined Option D variants that reflect the corrected architectural reading.

## Tier A — Well-documented facts (citable)

Each item below is corroborated by ≥2 independent secondary sources or by a primary archival reproduction. Sources at the bottom.

### ~14th century — At least two *brassines franches* in Durbuy

Beerplanet.net summarises Durbuy's medieval brewing landscape: by the 14th century, Durbuy already had at least two free brewery houses (*brassines franches*) — one at the Halle aux Blés (the famous half-timbered "Spanish House", classified Walloon major heritage), and the brassine *Au Chesne* that Marckloff would later operate from his 1560 building. Beer in this period: barley-based, sometimes with spelt; *Hougarde* style mentioned.

### *circa* 1560 — Philippe Marckloff founds his brewery at La Ferme au Chesne

- **What**: Marckloff builds the stone manor (*gentilhommière*) at the entrance of Durbuy, opposite what is now the Parc des Topiaires, on the banks of the Ourthe. He establishes the second *brasserie franche* of the medieval town, named *Au Chesne*. The site is a working brewery, mill, and household for him and his sons.
- **Who Marckloff was**: **maître d'hôtel of Antoine de Metz**, *gouverneur de la seigneurie de Durbuy*. **He was a noble household official — chief steward of the seigneurie's governor — not an independent guild artisan**. Antoine de Metz is a separate noble lineage, almost certainly Lorraine-based; he is not connected to the Schetz/d'Ursel family who would not arrive at Durbuy for another 68 years.
- **Detail**: The mill was originally powered by dogs (*moulin motorisé par des chiens*). The Marckloff family operated the brewery for ~50 years (so until ~1610). They also held municipal offices, including mayor of Durbuy.
- **Date conflict, transparently**: 1560 (La Libre, Cirkwi, Wikipedia, Cercle Terre de Durbuy, Beerplanet) vs 1569 (L'Avenir, likely the building construction date). Use *vers 1560* / *circa 1560* in copy.
- **Sources**: La Libre [1], Wikipedia NL [2], Polybeer [3], L'Avenir [4], Cirkwi [5], Mini-Ardenne [6], Beerplanet [14].

### 1609 — Nicolas de Blier appointed prévôt of Durbuy by the Archdukes

- **By whom**: **The Archdukes Albert and Isabella** (Habsburg sovereigns of the Spanish Netherlands), via letters patent of **28 April 1609**, when the d'Oostfrise engagère of Durbuy was wound up and the seigneurie returned briefly to direct Habsburg authority.
- **Crucial implication**: De Blier was **not** appointed by the Schetz/d'Ursel family. He took office 19 years before they acquired the seigneurie. When Antoine Schetz arrived in 1628, de Blier was already the established administrator and continued in his role.
- **De Blier's roles**: Capitaine, prévôt, gruyer et receveur — head of administration, justice, woodlands, and revenues of the entire Durbuy seigneurie.
- **Origins**: Father Henri de Blier, seigneur d'Hébronval. Letters of nobility 20 July 1618. Acquired seigneuries of Blier and Hazeille (1611), and of Wallay and Reppe.
- **Quarters at the château**: Confirmed locally (per the user) and consistent with the published source: *"Ils [les seigneurs engagistes] ne vivent pas à Durbuy. Ils se font représenter par un prévôt qui occupe généralement une partie du château"* (Cirkwi). The prévôt traditionally occupied part of the château.
- **Authoritative archival source**: *L'Engagère de la Terre et Seigneurie de Durbuy en 1628* (PDF on Wéris-info) [7] reproduces the 1626 declaration in full, including the biographical notice on de Blier.

### 1628 — The Schetz/d'Ursel family acquires Durbuy

- **30 January 1628**: **Baron Antoine Schetz de Grobendonck** takes the engagère of Durbuy for **40,000 marks of gold** — the family's own narrative on chateaudedurbuy.be calls this *"le Marché du siècle"* (the deal of the century in Belgium).
- **Who he was**: A great captain who heroically defended Bois-le-Duc (Hertogenbosch) and Louvain against the Dutch armies. Member of the Council of War, master of camp of a Liège infantry regiment, governor of Bois-le-Duc.
- **Why he came**: *"Il vient à Durbuy pour redresser le domaine"* — to restore/reform the domain. He favoured the installation of the Récollets and Récollectines (religious orders) at Durbuy.
- **Family lineage**: The Schetz family had taken the d'Ursel name in 1617 by adoption (Conrard Schetz adopted by Barbara van Ursel). Antoine is from the Schetz/Grobendonck branch; the senior d'Ursel branch inherits Durbuy in 1726 from his great-grandson Hubert Schetz (who died without heirs).
- **Sources**: Château de Durbuy official PDF "1628, une nouvelle ère" [15], Wéris-info engagère [7], Wikipedia FR Maison d'Ursel [10], chateau-fort-manoir-chateau.eu [9].

### 1630 — The brewery passes from the Marckloff family to Nicolas de Blier

- The Marckloff property at La Ferme au Chesne passes to Nicolas de Blier, who is already the prévôt of Durbuy. The brewery continues under his authority.
- **This transition happens under the Schetz/d'Ursel seigneurie** (Antoine Schetz had been seigneur for ~2 years), but the d'Ursel/Schetz family did not orchestrate the transition — they inherited an existing administrative structure.
- **Direct documentation**: Joseph Bernard's posthumous article *"La Ferme au Chesne à Durbuy, gentilhommière des Marckloff et de Nicolas de Blier (XVIème et XVIIème siècles)"*, in Cercle Terre de Durbuy revue no. 135 [8].
- **Sources**: Cirkwi [5], L'Avenir [4], Cercle Terre de Durbuy [8].

### 1675 — The original château is demolished by French troops

- **10–12 August 1675**: French commander **Sandar de Louvignies** demolishes and dismantles the château during the Maastricht campaign of the Franco-Dutch wars.
- **20 October 1675**: Coup de grâce delivered by capitaine de Courville.
- **Earlier damage**: Already destroyed in part during the 1636 Franco-Dutch wars.
- **Pre-history of destructions**: The château had also been razed in 1484/1492 (La Marck wars), in 1156 (fire), and ravaged in 1237 and 1317 (Limbourg succession wars).
- **What survived**: Foundations, cellars, lower walls cut into the rocky outcrop. The outer walls, towers, and habitation levels were destroyed.
- **Sources**: Château de Durbuy official PDF "1628, une nouvelle ère" [15], Cirkwi [16].

### 1731 — The current château is rebuilt

- **By the Duke d'Ursel** (Conrard-Albert, first Duke d'Ursel and Hoboken from 1716).
- Rebuilt as a *château de plaisance* (pleasure castle) — no longer a fortress.
- Built on the surviving substructure (foundations and cellars from the pre-1675 château).
- **Source**: Château de Durbuy official PDF [15], Cirkwi [16], Wikipedia FR Durbuy [17].

### 1756 — D'Ursel family acquires full ownership

- For **40,000 gold florins**, the Emperor cedes the totality of the property of the Terre de Durbuy.
- This converts the engagère (held since 1628) into outright ownership.
- **Source**: Château de Durbuy official PDF [15].

### 1880–1882 — Neo-gothic modernisation

- By **Comtesse Auguste d'Ursel** (née Marie-Camille de Croix, widow of Comte Auguste).
- Architect: **Thirian de Namur**, advised by **Viollet-le-Duc**.
- Gives the château its current neo-gothic appearance.
- **Source**: Château de Durbuy official PDF [15], Wikipedia FR Durbuy [17].

### 1989 — The Trine brothers revive the Marckloff brewery

- **Founders**: Brothers **Jacques** and **Michel Trine** of Durbuy. Initial collaboration with **Dany Prignon** of Brasserie Fantôme.
- **Heritage beer**: Flagship amber beer named **Marckloff** (6.5% ABV) in direct homage to Philippe Marckloff.
- **Continued**: Jacques Trine died in 2011; Michel Trine and his sister-in-law Charline Paulus continued. Site closed briefly October 2013, reopened 2014.
- **Sources**: Wikipedia NL [2], Polybeer [3], Beer Me! [11], La Libre [1], Beerplanet [14].

### 2020 — Brasserie du Château de Durbuy is established in the anciennes écuries

- **Founders**: **Comte Jean-Michel d'Ursel** (current d'Ursel family member, son of Comte Adrien d'Ursel 1926–2006) and **Marc Leclercq**.
- **Location**: The brewery moves into the *anciennes écuries* (former stables) of the Château de Durbuy. Address: **Rue du Comte Théodule d'Ursel, 2 — 6940 Durbuy**.
- **Operating entity**: "La brasserie du château de Durbuy, srl" — confirmed in Belgian public mandate records (Cumuleo).
- **Position**: Continuation/relocation of the brewing tradition. TerroirLux: *"Voorheen gekend onder de naam 'Brasserie de la Ferme au Chêne'"* — formerly known as Brasserie de la Ferme au Chêne.
- **First time in 460 years that a member of the d'Ursel family directly operates a brewery on the domain.** They had been the seigneurial authority over Durbuy brewing since 1628 — this is the first direct operation.
- **Sources**: TerroirLux [12], Cumuleo [13].

## Modern brewery — sourced canonical facts

These are canonical brand facts sourced directly from the brand owner / brewmaster (Lars Kristel, 10 May 2026). They supersede any contradicting information from secondary sources.

### Production volume

- **2024 production: 21 × 500-litre brews = 10,500 litres = 105 hectolitres (hL).**
- This is genuinely *petites séries* by Belgian brewing standards. For context:
  - Trappist Westvleteren ≈ 50,000 hL/year (the most "exclusive" Trappist).
  - The historical Brasserie La Ferme au Chêne under the Trine brothers ≈ 80 hL/year (per Beer Me!).
  - Major Belgian commercial breweries: thousands to hundreds of thousands of hL/year.
- The brewery's site copy claim of *"production limitée"* and *"distribution confidentielle"* is now empirically defensible. 105 hL/year is small enough that the limited / confidential framing reads as truth, not marketing.

### Brewmaster

- **Lars Kristel** — official brewmaster of the Brasserie du Château de Durbuy since **January 2026**. Previously brewery consultant in 2024 (the year of the 105 hL production above).
- **Born**: 11 October 1985 in Naarden, Netherlands. Dutch by birth, brewmaster of a Belgian estate.
- **Strategic note**: a Dutch brewmaster running a Belgian estate brewery is a bona fide editorial story — quietly cosmopolitan, technically authoritative, slightly unexpected. It's the kind of detail the Le Domaine sub-page can carry without it becoming a personality cult.

### Implications for site copy

These facts unlock several things that were previously vague:

1. The site can credibly say "*Vingt-et-un brassins en 2024. Cent cinq hectolitres. Distribution confidentielle.*" That's a specific, verifiable, restraint-coded line in the brand voice.
2. The brewmaster has a name and a date — the Le Domaine sub-page can cite Lars Kristel as *maître brasseur* without it feeling like personality marketing. One sentence: "*Maître brasseur depuis 2026 : Lars Kristel.*"
3. The brewmaster credit also strengthens the heritage thread: a trained outside professional has chosen to brew here. That choice is itself a signal of the place's quality.

### Open question — to resolve before printing

- **2025 and projected 2026 production volumes**: not yet shared. If similar to 2024 (≈100 hL), the "vingt-et-un brassins / cent hectolitres" line is robust. If significantly different, copy needs to update.
- **Whether Lars wants his name printed on the site at all** — this is a brand-voice call. Some estate brewers prefer anonymity (the place is the protagonist). Others foreground the brewmaster (Aman often names its head chef; Margaux always names its régisseur). **Resolved 10 May 2026**: Lars's name is reserved for the future Le Domaine sub-page, NOT on the homepage. ## Tier B — Plausible inferences (well-corroborated, not directly cited)

### Continuous brewing tradition

The brewery has operated, with one verified pause in the 19th–20th centuries (it functioned as a farm — the Ferme au Chesne stopped operating as a farm in 1971), at the same Durbuy estate since 1560. The Trine brothers' 1989 revival was explicit homage to the Marckloff lineage, and the 2020 d'Ursel-Leclercq move to the château is described in trade sources as a continuation of the same operation. **The phrase "continuous brewing heritage at Durbuy since 1560" is defensible**, with the caveat that there were dormant periods.

### The Marckloff–de Blier–d'Ursel chain

While no single source connects all four parties (Marckloff, de Blier, d'Ursel, modern brasserie) in one narrative, the historical pieces fit cleanly:

- Marckloff brewed in the 16th century at Ferme au Chesne.
- de Blier (prévôt with quarters at the château) inherited the brewery in 1630.
- The château later (1726/1756) became d'Ursel property.
- The d'Ursel family relocated brewing into the château stables in 2020.

**The phrase "the brewery returns to the château" is therefore poetic license, not historical claim** — there was never a brewery in the château proper before 2020 (with the noted exception of the dependency building at the petite anticlinal, per the Comte's testimony — a separate building). But "the château has been associated with Durbuy brewing since the prévôt of 1630" is *defensible inference*.

### Beer lineup mapping

The terroirlux.be entry and the brewery's own bottle line suggest the current four beers are:

| Bottle label | Likely heritage beer | ABV | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| Blonde du Château | (new, 2020 brand) | 6,4° | Estate signature |
| Bohemian Pilsner | Pilsner Bohémienne (existed under La Ferme au Chêne) | 4,6° | Light pilsner |
| IPA | Heir of *Durbuy-Hanyu Sorachi Ace* (2015 one-off) | 5,3° | Houblon Sorachi |
| Amber Ale | **Marckloff** (the heritage beer since 1989) | 6,2° | Almost certainly the Marckloff in English-style label |

The current English-style labels ("IPA", "Amber Ale") are likely the modern bottle line; the back label or the brewery's own naming may still use *Marckloff* and *Sorachi Ace* for connoisseurs. This needs confirmation. **If the Amber Ale is in fact the Marckloff renamed, this is a major heritage detail that could anchor the beer page.**

### The "anciennes écuries" reference

The current site's reference to *les anciennes écuries du château* ("the former stables of the château") matches the established physical location. The stables are part of the 17th-century main château complex. **No further research needed — the phrase is accurate.**
